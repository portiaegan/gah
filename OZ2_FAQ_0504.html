What are Opportunity Zones?

The basics of the federal program — what it is, how it works, and who can use it.

What is an Opportunity Zone? ▼

An Opportunity Zone (OZ) is a low-income census tract designated by a state governor and certified by the U.S. Treasury. The designation unlocks a federal tax incentive that allows investors with capital gains to defer and reduce their tax liability by reinvesting those gains into designated OZ communities through a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF).

The program was created by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017. Its goal is to direct private capital into economically distressed communities that might otherwise struggle to attract investment.

What is a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF)? ▼

A QOF is an investment vehicle — structured as a partnership or corporation — that holds at least 90% of its assets in Qualified Opportunity Zone property. Investors with eligible capital gains invest in a QOF to access the OZ tax benefits. QOFs then deploy that capital into projects or businesses located in designated OZ tracts.

QOFs can be large national funds or locally structured vehicles. They are established with the IRS and are responsible for ensuring investments meet OZ program requirements.

What tax benefits does the OZ program offer investors? ▼

Under OZ 2.0, investors who reinvest eligible capital gains into a QOF can receive:

  • Temporary deferral of the original capital gains tax until the investment is sold or December 31, 2028 — whichever comes first
  • Partial reduction of the original deferred gain (details pending final Treasury regulations)
  • Permanent exclusion of gains from the OZ investment itself if held for at least 10 years
The specific benefit levels under OZ 2.0 are subject to final IRS and Treasury guidance. Investors and developers should consult qualified tax counsel before making investment decisions.
What types of projects qualify for OZ investment? ▼

OZ investment can flow into two main categories:

  • Real estate projects — new construction (original use) or substantial improvement of existing property within an OZ. Substantial improvement requires doubling the adjusted basis of the building within 30 months of acquisition.
  • Operating businesses — businesses that maintain at least 70% of their tangible assets within an OZ tract. This includes startups, expanding businesses, and businesses relocating into the zone.

There are important restrictions: vacant land and certain business types (golf courses, country clubs, massage parlors, hot tub facilities, suntan facilities, racetracks, casinos, liquor stores, and self-storage) are excluded or face special limitations.

OZ 2.0 — What's New

How the new program differs from OZ 1.0 and what's changing for investors, developers, and communities.

What is OZ 2.0 and how is it different from the original program? ▼

OZ 2.0 is a reauthorization of the Opportunity Zones program, enacted as part of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act of 2025. It extends and updates the original 2017 program with several key changes:

  • New tract designations — states select a new set of census tracts, replacing OZ 1.0 designations. Designations take effect January 1, 2027.
  • Updated eligibility criteria — the program expands eligible tract types modestly, including certain rural communities.
  • Reporting requirements — OZ 2.0 includes new transparency and reporting requirements for QOFs, designed to improve accountability and measure community impact.
  • Program continuity — the core tax incentive structure (deferral, reduction, exclusion) is preserved, though specific benefit levels are subject to final Treasury guidance.
Final regulations for OZ 2.0 are still being developed by the IRS and Treasury. Some program details may evolve. Check IRS.gov for the latest guidance.
What happened to OZ 1.0 tracts? Do they still exist? ▼

OZ 1.0 designations expire December 31, 2026. After that date, investments in former OZ 1.0 tracts that are not re-designated under OZ 2.0 will no longer qualify for new OZ tax benefits. Existing investments made before the expiration date generally retain their OZ 1.0 benefits under the terms in place when they were made.

Some OZ 1.0 tracts may be re-nominated and re-designated under OZ 2.0, but there is no automatic carryover — each tract must go through the new nomination and selection process.

When do OZ 2.0 investments become eligible? ▼

Investments in newly designated OZ 2.0 tracts become eligible for the OZ tax incentive starting January 1, 2027. Georgia's nomination deadline is August 15, 2026, with the state selection deadline following in late September 2026 — aligned with the federal deadline of September 29, 2026 for governors to submit designations to Treasury.

Georgia's Approach

How Georgia is thinking about OZ 2.0, what the state is prioritizing, and what happened under OZ 1.0.

How many tracts can Georgia designate? ▼

Georgia has 942 eligible census tracts and may designate up to 236 as OZ 2.0 zones — approximately 25% of eligible tracts. The state is using a nomination process to identify the strongest candidates before making final selections.

What is Georgia prioritizing in its OZ 2.0 selections? ▼

Georgia is looking for tracts where OZ designation would make a real difference — where private capital is close but not quite there without the incentive. The state's priorities include:

  • Tracts with credible, near-ready investment opportunities
  • Projects that align with Georgia's economic development goals — job creation, housing, advanced industries, rural development, and community revitalization
  • Geographic diversity, ensuring communities across the state have access to the program
  • Tracts where OZ designation closes a genuine financing gap or accelerates timing

This is an economic development initiative, not a commercial subsidy program. The state is not looking to simply designate the most distressed tracts — it is looking for tracts where investment is investable and where OZ capital will actually land.

What did OZ 1.0 accomplish in Georgia? ▼

Georgia designated 260 census tracts under OZ 1.0 in 2018. Nationally, the program catalyzed an estimated $100 billion in private investment, supporting over 300,000 housing units. In Georgia, confirmed OZ 1.0 investments include:

  • Build-to-Rent Housing, Decatur & Augusta Region — Pinnacle Partners and Trilogy Investment raised $60 million in OZ equity (10-year hold structure) to develop 45 market-rate townhomes in Decatur and 245 workforce housing units in the Augusta region. OZ equity served as the primary equity layer.
  • Atlanta-Area Multifamily & Mixed-Use — Atlanta ranked among the top U.S. metros for OZ equity deployment, with over $635 million raised for Atlanta-area projects in 2023 (CoStar). Multiple QOFs listed Georgia as an active investment geography focused on multifamily and workforce housing.
  • South & Southwest Atlanta (Invest Atlanta) — Invest Atlanta layered OZ equity with local tax abatement and gap funding to advance housing and mixed-use redevelopment in City of Atlanta OZ tracts. Projects moved forward only after OZ equity commitments were secured.
Will DCA provide any support to help communities attract OZ investment? ▼

Georgia is committed to making its OZ 2.0 program investor-friendly and not just a list of designated tracts. The state is exploring tools to support deal flow, including:

  • A project pipeline tool / marketplace to connect project sponsors, QOFs, and investors
  • Technical assistance for communities preparing nominations and packaging investment opportunities
  • Outreach to fund managers and investors to match Georgia opportunities with capital

Details on these programs will be announced following the designation process. Communities can share feedback on what would be most helpful in the Program Feedback section of the nomination form.

Who Should Nominate

Anyone can submit a nomination — but strong nominations come from people close to the community or the project.

Who can submit a nomination? ▼

Any individual or organization can submit a nomination. The strongest nominations typically come from:

  • Local and county governments — nominating tracts based on community plans and economic development priorities
  • Economic development organizations — regional planning commissions, EDOs, and chambers familiar with local investment conditions
  • Developers and project sponsors — who have a specific site or project in mind and can speak to investment readiness
  • Business owners — looking to expand, relocate, or establish operations within an eligible tract
  • Qualified Opportunity Funds — actively looking for investable projects and tracts

You do not need to be a developer or have a specific project to nominate. A city government or community organization nominating a tract for its economic development potential is equally valid.

Can I nominate more than one tract? ▼

Yes. You may submit nominations for multiple tracts. Each tract requires its own form. There is no hard cap on the number of nominations you can submit.

That said, quality matters more than quantity. A focused nomination that clearly explains why a specific tract deserves designation — with evidence of investment readiness — will be more persuasive than a large volume of generic nominations. We encourage you to prioritize your strongest opportunities.

Can a developer or investor nominate a tract for their own project? ▼

Yes. This is explicitly encouraged. If you have a specific project that would be enabled by OZ designation, you are exactly the kind of nominator the state wants to hear from. Being transparent about your relationship to the project — and demonstrating that the project is real, ready, and would genuinely benefit from OZ capital — strengthens your nomination.

This is not a developer-driven program in the sense that the state is simply rubber-stamping developer requests. Nominations will be evaluated on community fit, investment readiness, and alignment with Georgia's economic development priorities.

How Nominations Are Used

What DCA does with nominations and how selections are made.

How will DCA use nomination submissions to select tracts? ▼

Nominations inform — but do not determine — the state's final selections. DCA will review all submissions and use them alongside its own analysis to identify the strongest candidates. Factors considered include:

  • Investment readiness and project credibility
  • Evidence that OZ designation would materially change outcomes (close a gap, accelerate timing)
  • Alignment with Georgia's economic development priorities
  • Geographic diversity across the state
  • Poverty rate and distress indicators of the tract

Submitting a nomination does not guarantee selection. Multiple nominations for the same tract may strengthen the case for that tract.

Is my nomination public? ▼

Nomination submissions are used by DCA for the selection process. The state may publish aggregate information about nominations received. Individual nomination details — including project descriptions, financial information, and contact details — are treated as confidential and not shared publicly without consent.

Will I be notified whether my nominated tract was selected? ▼

Yes. DCA will notify nominators once selections are finalized. Final designations will also be published publicly following the federal deadline of September 29, 2026. A confirmation copy of your nomination will be emailed to you immediately upon submission.

What Makes a Strong Nomination

Practical guidance on what to focus on and what to avoid.

What does a strong nomination look like? ▼

The strongest nominations share three characteristics:

  • The designation materially changes outcomes — OZ equity would close a genuine financing gap, accelerate a project that would otherwise take years longer, or attract capital that wouldn't otherwise consider this location.
  • The tract is genuinely investable — there's infrastructure, market demand, site control or potential, and some evidence that the private sector sees opportunity here — even if it hasn't fully committed yet.
  • The investment advances community goals — the project or development type aligns with what residents, local governments, and the state actually want to see happen in that community.

Project-level detail significantly strengthens a nomination. Nominations that describe a specific project, sponsor, site, and financing approach are weighted more heavily than general tract nominations.

What are common mistakes to avoid? ▼
  • Nominating every eligible tract in your county — this dilutes the strength of your actual priorities. Submit nominations where you have genuine evidence of investment opportunity.
  • Assuming distress alone is sufficient — the state is looking for tracts where OZ designation will actually attract investment, not simply the most disadvantaged areas.
  • Vague project descriptions — "mixed-use development" without a sponsor, site, or scale is much weaker than a named project with a developer attached.
  • Ignoring OZ eligibility rules — if your project involves substantial improvement of an existing building, confirm you can double the adjusted basis within 30 months. If it's a business, confirm the 70% asset test is achievable.
Does my project need to be shovel-ready to nominate? ▼

No. Projects at every stage of readiness are welcome — from early concept to shovel-ready. However, the more advanced your project, the stronger your nomination will be. A project in the permitting stage with site control and identified financing is far more persuasive than a concept with no site and no sponsor.

If you're nominating based on a tract's investment potential rather than a specific project, that's also valid. Be clear about why you believe the tract is investable and what signals of interest you're seeing.

Completing the Form

Tips on specific form questions and how to navigate the nomination process.

How do I find the GEOID for the census tract I want to nominate? ▼

The GEOID is the unique 11-digit identifier for a census tract. On the nomination form, you can select your county first and the tract list will filter to eligible tracts in that county. If you don't know which tract a specific address falls within, use the "Search by address" link on the Census Tract page of the form.

You can also use the Census Bureau's geocoder tool at geocoding.geo.census.gov or the FFIEC's Geocomapfree tool to look up tract information by address.

What's the difference between the two nomination paths — "tract investment potential" vs. "specific project in mind"? ▼

Tract investment potential is for nominators who believe a tract deserves OZ designation based on its economic development characteristics — market fundamentals, infrastructure, location, development conditions — but who do not have a specific project attached. Local governments and EDOs often nominate this way.

Specific project in mind is for nominators who can describe a defined project, business expansion, or development opportunity that OZ designation would directly enable. This path collects more detail about the project, site, developer, readiness, and financing.

Both paths are valid. Nominations with specific project detail are generally stronger because they demonstrate that investment is not just possible but imminent.

What should I upload in the Supporting Documents section? ▼

Upload a project prospectus or overview document — a concise summary of the project, investment opportunity, and community impact. One-pagers and brief excerpts are preferred over full plans or lengthy studies.

If you don't have a formal prospectus, a brief memo, executive summary, or site plan excerpt is fine. The goal is to give DCA reviewers enough context to understand the project quickly.

You can also add up to three links to relevant external resources — a local comprehensive plan, redevelopment study, developer website, or other supporting material.

Can I save my nomination and come back to it later? ▼

The current version of this form does not support draft saving. We recommend completing the form in one session, or drafting your answers in a separate document before entering them. You can also submit a nomination with partial information and follow up with supplemental material — contact DCA at [email address] to do so.

Draft-saving functionality may be added before the August 15, 2026 deadline. Check back or contact DCA for updates.

What Happens After Nomination

The process from submission to designation to investment eligibility.

What is the timeline from nomination to investment eligibility? ▼
  • August 15, 2026 — Georgia nomination deadline
  • September 29, 2026 — Federal deadline for Georgia to submit designations to Treasury
  • Late 2026 — Treasury certifies designations and publishes final OZ 2.0 tract list
  • January 1, 2027 — OZ 2.0 investment eligibility begins; new QOF investments in designated tracts qualify for OZ tax benefits
If my tract is designated, what comes next for my project? ▼

Designation opens the door — it doesn't automatically bring capital. Once your tract is designated, the work of attracting OZ investment begins:

  • Your project needs to be packaged in a way that is attractive to QOFs and OZ investors
  • You'll need to connect with Qualified Opportunity Funds that are interested in your project type and geography
  • The project sponsor or developer will need to work with legal and financial advisors to structure a compliant OZ investment

Georgia's DCA and economic development partners will provide resources to help connect projects with investors following designation. Details will be announced after the selection process.

What if my tract is not selected? ▼

With 942 eligible tracts and only 236 to be designated, most nominated tracts will not be selected. If your tract is not designated, OZ investment will not be available — but many other state and federal programs remain available for economic development in eligible communities.

DCA will provide information on alternative programs and resources for communities whose tracts are not selected. Contact DCA at [email address] for more information.
